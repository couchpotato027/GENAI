# Pedagogical Knowledge Base for Exam Questions

## Assessment Purpose

### Formative Assessment & Recall
- **Knowledge Checks**: Easy questions with high pass rates serve a valid formative purpose — verifying that students have acquired baseline knowledge before progressing to higher-order tasks.
- **Low Discrimination is Expected**: For recall-level items, most students answer correctly. Low discrimination does not indicate flawed design; it reflects the assessment purpose.
- **Optional Upgrade Path**: If the assessment goal shifts from recall to analysis, consider rephrasing questions to require explanation, comparison, or application rather than simple retrieval.
- **Bloom's Lower Tiers**: Questions at the 'Remember' and 'Understand' levels are foundational and necessary, even though they produce minimal variance.

## Discrimination Issues
### Negative Discrimination
- **Check Validity**: Ensure the answer key is correct. High performers may be selecting the "correct" answer which is keyed wrong.
- **Remove Ambiguity**: If high performers are distracted by a plausible distractor, clarify the distinction between the correct answer and that distractor.
- **Review Content**: Ensure the question doesn't rely on misconceptions that high performers have overcome but low performers haven't.

### Low Discrimination
- **Increase Complexity**: The question may be too easy (ceiling effect) or random (guessing). Add nuance to require deeper understanding.
- **Refine Distractors**: Make distractors more plausible to students with partial knowledge, so only those with mastery select the correct answer.
- **Alignment**: Ensure the question tests the specific learning outcome intended, not general intelligence or test-taking skills.

### Hard but Well-Designed
- **Sub-parts**: Break the question into sub-parts with explicit marks for each step, allowing students to demonstrate partial mastery.
- **Explicit Assumptions**: State all assumptions, given values, and constraints clearly in the question stem so difficulty comes from the concept, not from guessing the setup.
- **Mark Distribution**: Provide a clear mark distribution (e.g., "part a: 2 marks, part b: 3 marks") so students know how to allocate effort.
- **Scaffolding**: For multi-step problems, consider providing an intermediate result or hint that allows students to proceed even if they miss an earlier step.
- **Partial Credit**: Design the rubric to award partial credit for correct methodology even if the final answer is wrong.

## Difficulty Issues
### Extremely High Difficulty
- **Scaffolding**: Check if the question requires multiple leaps of logic. Break it down or provide necessary info in the stem.
- **Prerequisites**: Ensure the content has been adequately covered.
- **Clarity**: Verify that the difficulty comes from the concept, not confusing wording.

### Extremely Low Difficulty
- **Trivia**: Avoid simple recall if the goal is higher-order thinking.
- **Clues**: Remove grammatical cues or associations that give away the answer (e.g., matching singular/plural).
- **Misconceptions**: Design the question to specifically target common student errors rather than just asking for a definition.

## Clarity & Alignment
- **Stem Quality**: The stem should present a clear problem. A student should know what is being asked before reading the options.
- **Option Homogeneity**: All options should be parallel in structure, length, and content type.
- **Avoid Negatives**: Avoid "NOT" or "EXCEPT" questions unless absolutely necessary, as they increase cognitive load unrelated to content mastery.
